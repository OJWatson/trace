TRACE

Temporal and Regional Analysis of Conflict Events

Semi-Mechanistic Bayesian Models for Conflict Casualty Analysis

Imperial College London

The Challenge

During active conflicts, reliable casualty data is critical for humanitarian response—but often unavailable or incomplete.
  • Under-reporting: True tolls often exceed official figures
  • Multiple data streams: Events, hospital records, mortality reports
  • Spatial complexity: Casualties distributed across hospitals
  • Temporal delays: Deaths occur days/weeks after injury
  • Uncertainty: Need principled quantification for decision-making
Goal: Integrate multiple data sources to estimate casualties and forecast under scenarios

TRACE: Semi-mechanistic approach—explicit casualty generation process + statistical inference

Inspiration: Epidemic Modeling

TRACE adapts methods from COVID-19 modeling (Flaxman et al., 2020):

Epidemiology Conflict
Susceptible population Population at risk
Infections (Rt) Conflict events (intensity)
Hospitalizations Hospital admissions
Deaths (with delays) Deaths (immediate + delayed)
Age-stratified contacts Spatial allocation to hospitals

Key difference: Conflict events are exogenous (driven by military operations), not self-propagating like infections

Three Components:

  1. Conflict Event Process — Discrete events with locations & times
  2. Spatial Allocation — Casualties distributed to hospitals by distance
  3. Temporal Dynamics — Delay from injury to death

Component 1: Spatial Allocation

Casualties from event e allocated to hospital h based on distance:

we,h = exp(−de,h / ℓ) / Σh' exp(−de,h' / ℓ)
  • ℓ = spatial length scale (inferred from data)
  • Closer hospitals receive more casualties
  • Accounts for accessibility, infrastructure, security
Enables: Hospital-specific forecasting, identifying underserved areas, optimizing mobile medical unit placement

Component 2: Temporal Dynamics

Deaths combine immediate and delayed components:

δt = μi · |Et| + plate · Σk It−k · fk

Parameters

  • μi: Immediate deaths per event
  • plate: Hospital fatality rate
  • fk: Delay distribution

Delay Distribution

Based on trauma literature:

  • Day 1: 50%
  • Day 2: 30%
  • Day 3: 15%
  • Day 4+: 5%

Component 3: Observation Model

Hospital injuries and deaths modeled as Poisson random variables:

Ht,h ~ Poisson(λt,h)

Dt ~ Poisson(δt)

Where expected injuries depend on events and spatial weights:

λt,h = μw · Σe∈Et we,h

μw = average wounded per event (varies by conflict type: airstrikes vs. ground combat)

Why Bayesian?

Challenges

  • Under-reporting: Unknown ascertainment rates
  • Attribution: Which injuries caused which deaths?
  • Confounding: Multiple interventions

Bayesian Advantages

  • Regularization: Informative priors
  • Uncertainty: Full posteriors
  • Hierarchical: Partial pooling
Implementation: NumPyro + JAX for fast, GPU-accelerated MCMC (NUTS sampler)

Model Parameters

Parameter Meaning Prior
μw Wounded per event Exp(0.3) → mean ~3.3
μi Immediate deaths per event Exp(0.3) → mean ~3.3
plate Hospital fatality rate Beta(2, 10) → mean ~17%
ℓ Spatial length scale Exp(1.0) → mean ~1°

Priors are weakly informative, informed by conflict literature. Can be adjusted based on context.

Applications

1. Parameter Estimation
Estimate casualties per event, hospital fatality rates, spatial patterns
2. Model Assessment
Posterior predictive checks, identify model misspecification
3. Forecasting
Short-term (resource allocation) and long-term (policy) projections
4. Scenario Analysis
Ceasefire, escalation, intervention effects
5. In-Silico Experiments
Sensitivity analysis, study design, validation
6. Humanitarian Planning
Hospital capacity, supply distribution, evacuation planning

Extensions & Future Work

  • Time-varying parameters: Allow μw, μi to change (weapon changes, medical improvements)
  • Covariates: Intervention indicators, conflict type effects
  • Hierarchical structure: Partial pooling across regions/conflicts
  • Overdispersion: Negative Binomial for extra-Poisson variation
  • Missing data: Imputation within model structure
  • Population displacement: Dynamic population at risk
  • Self-exciting events: Endogenous event generation

Summary

TRACE provides a principled framework for conflict casualty analysis:
  • Semi-mechanistic: Explicit casualty generation + statistical inference
  • Multi-stream: Integrates events, hospital, and mortality data
  • Spatial: Distance-based allocation to hospitals
  • Temporal: Delay distributions from injury to death
  • Bayesian: Full uncertainty quantification
  • Actionable: Scenario-based forecasting for policy
